# Crown Key Realty — Email Excellence Rubric
## CrownKey Email Guardian — Scoring Specification v1.0

**Document Purpose:** This rubric defines what a high-quality email looks like for each recipient type. The Email Guardian AI will score outgoing emails on a scale of 1–10 using this rubric. Scores are for internal improvement only — they are never shown to recipients.

**The Core Question:** Does this email leave the recipient feeling well-served?

A well-served tenant feels: respected, informed, clear on what to do next.
A well-served owner feels: confident their investment is in expert hands, clear on what is being asked of them.
A well-served vendor feels: briefed completely, able to do the job without a follow-up call.

**How to Use This Rubric:**
The AI evaluates each email on the dimensions listed for that recipient type, generates a score per dimension, and averages them to produce a final 1–10 score. Scores below 6 trigger a polish pass before the email is offered to the user. Scores below 4 trigger a warning flag.

## Rubric 1: Tenant Emails (Score 1–10)

### Scoring Dimensions

Score each dimension 1–10, then average for the overall score.

1. **Tone** — Does the email sound Friendly, Firm, and Professional? (see tone-profiles.md)
2. **Clarity** — Is the core message immediately understandable?
3. **Empathy** — Does the email acknowledge the human being receiving it?
4. **Completeness** — Does the email include everything the tenant needs to act?
5. **Action Items** — Are the required actions and deadlines stated plainly?

---

### Score Band Descriptions — Tenant

**Score 1–3: Fails the Tenant**

An email in this range has one or more of these problems:
- The tone is cold, threatening, or dismissive. The tenant feels like a number, not a person.
- The email is factually incomplete — the tenant cannot understand what to do, who to contact, or by when.
- The language is confusing, jargon-heavy, or grammatically broken to the point of being hard to read.
- The email is unnecessarily alarming for its content (e.g., using the word "legal action" in a first-contact late rent reminder).
- The email has no human greeting or sign-off. It reads like a form letter generated by a machine.
- The email makes an accusation without evidence ("You clearly did not clean the unit").
- The email references potential Fair Housing concerns (flagged separately, but also affects tone score).

What the tenant feels: annoyed, disrespected, or confused. Likely to call the office demanding clarification, or to escalate the situation.

**Score 4–6: Safe but Mediocre**

An email in this range meets the minimum bar but misses the opportunity to be great:
- The tone is neutral — not warm, not cold. Reads like a form letter.
- The required information is present but buried in long paragraphs.
- There is a generic greeting but no specific acknowledgment of the tenant's situation.
- The email accomplishes its legal purpose (delivering a notice, confirming a date) but does not build the relationship.
- Action items are listed but the deadline language is vague ("soon," "as quickly as possible," "at your earliest convenience").
- The sign-off is generic ("Sincerely, Management").

What the tenant feels: okay — they got the information but the company doesn't feel like a great partner. Neutral impression.

**Score 7–8: Good — Professional Quality**

An email in this range is what Crown Key aims for in routine communications:
- Tone is warm but not casual. Reads like a competent human wrote it.
- The core message is in the first two sentences. The tenant knows what this is about immediately.
- Action items are clear: "Please [action] by [specific date]."
- There is a genuine human acknowledgment (tenant's name, a reference to their situation, or thanks for their patience).
- The email ends with an open door ("If you have questions, please reach out").
- Grammar and spelling are correct throughout.
- The sign-off includes the manager's name, not just "Management."

What the tenant feels: this is a professional company. I know what I need to do. I feel respected.

**Score 9–10: World Class — Tenant Feels Genuinely Cared For**

An email in this range goes beyond competent and delivers a relationship-building experience:
- The email is clearly written specifically for this tenant and this situation — not a template with a name swapped in.
- The tone strikes the perfect balance: firm when it needs to be, warm throughout.
- If the message is about a problem (late rent, violation), it acknowledges the difficulty without being preachy or condescending.
- It offers a specific next step and makes it easy to take: direct phone number, exact portal link, specific contact name.
- The email anticipates the tenant's next question and answers it preemptively.
- If relevant, it references the tenant's history positively ("You've been a great tenant and we want to work with you").
- The closing feels genuine, not formulaic.

What the tenant feels: Crown Key actually cares. This is a company worth staying with. Even if the message was about a problem, the relationship feels strengthened.

---

### Tenant Score — Dimension Examples

**Tone (1–10):**
- 1: "You are in violation of your lease and face eviction."
- 5: "We are writing to inform you that your rent payment is overdue."
- 10: "Hi [Name], I'm reaching out personally because I want to make sure we get this resolved together."

**Clarity (1–10):**
- 1: Three paragraphs of policy before stating what the tenant needs to do.
- 5: Main message is in the second paragraph.
- 10: Subject line and first sentence together tell the whole story.

**Empathy (1–10):**
- 1: No acknowledgment of the tenant as a person. Pure notice language.
- 5: Generic "we understand this may be inconvenient."
- 10: Specific acknowledgment ("We know this is a stressful time of year") or reference to tenant's positive history.

**Completeness (1–10):**
- 1: Missing key information (amount owed, deadline, or who to contact).
- 5: Core info present but no phone number or portal link.
- 10: Everything the tenant needs to act is in the email: amount, deadline, payment method, contact name and number.

**Action Items (1–10):**
- 1: No clear action requested.
- 5: Action mentioned but deadline is vague.
- 10: "Please submit payment of $[X] by [Date] via your AppFolio portal at [link]. If you cannot access the portal, call [Name] at [phone]."

## Rubric 2: Owner Emails (Score 1–10)

### Scoring Dimensions

1. **Expert Positioning** — Does the email sound like it's from the expert managing the investment?
2. **Tone** — Educated, Firm, Professional, Friendly (see tone-profiles.md)
3. **Clarity** — Is the situation immediately understandable?
4. **Recommendation Quality** — Does Crown Key offer a recommendation, or just report?
5. **Action Items** — Is the owner's required action clear and easy to take?

---

### Score Band Descriptions — Owner

**Score 1–3: Fails the Owner**

An email in this range damages the owner relationship:
- Crown Key sounds reactive, not proactive. The company is reporting a failure, not managing a situation.
- There is no recommendation. The owner is left to decide something Crown Key should be deciding.
- The email is alarming without being helpful ("We have a serious situation at your property").
- Numbers or dates are missing, forcing the owner to call for basic information.
- The tone is apologetic in a way that implies fault ("We're so sorry this happened").
- Crown Key's expertise is not evident anywhere in the email.

What the owner feels: concerned about whether they chose the right property manager. Might start shopping alternatives.

**Score 4–6: Safe but Mediocre**

- The situation is reported accurately but without context.
- A fix is mentioned but not explained or recommended — owner is left to approve something they don't fully understand.
- The email reads like a notification, not a management update.
- No reference to market context, legal implications, or long-term implications.
- Owner's ask is included but buried.

What the owner feels: informed, but not confident. Crown Key is doing the work but the owner isn't sure they're getting the best of it.

**Score 7–8: Good — Professional Quality**

- The situation, context, and Crown Key's response are all in the email.
- There is a clear recommendation with brief reasoning.
- The owner's ask is specific and easy to respond to (a simple reply or approval).
- The tone is confident but not dismissive.
- The email positions Crown Key as a partner in the investment, not a vendor reporting a problem.

What the owner feels: this is a well-run operation. Crown Key is on top of it. I'm glad I hired them.

**Score 9–10: World Class — Owner Feels Investment is in Expert Hands**

- The email demonstrates deep knowledge of this specific property, tenant, or situation — not just generic management-speak.
- The recommendation is specific, supported by reasoning, and reflects Crown Key's experience ("We've seen this at [similar context] and the better long-term move is...").
- The email protects the owner from something they didn't know to ask about (legal risk, deferred maintenance, market trend).
- The financial framing is strong: everything is tied to what it means for the investment.
- The owner's action is so clearly stated that responding takes 30 seconds.
- The sign-off is warm and relationship-forward.

What the owner feels: this is what excellent property management looks like. My investment is in the right hands.

---

### Owner Score — Dimension Examples

**Expert Positioning (1–10):**
- 1: "The tenant said there's a problem. We don't know what to do."
- 5: "We are looking into the repair and will get back to you."
- 10: "We've assessed the repair and recommend Option B — here's why it protects your investment long-term."

**Recommendation Quality (1–10):**
- 1: No recommendation offered.
- 5: "You could go with Option A or Option B."
- 10: "We recommend Option B. Here is our reasoning. If you agree, reply to this email and we will proceed today."

**Action Items (1–10):**
- 1: No clear ask from the owner.
- 5: "Please let us know what you'd like to do."
- 10: "Please reply with 'Approved' to authorize Option B at $[X]. We will begin scheduling within 24 hours of your response."

## Rubric 3: Vendor Emails (Score 1–10)

### Scoring Dimensions

1. **Completeness** — Does the vendor have everything they need to do the job without calling?
2. **Clarity** — Is the work required and the property address immediately clear?
3. **Structure** — Is the email organized (not a wall of text)?
4. **Actionability** — Does the email tell the vendor exactly what to do next?

---

### Score Band Descriptions — Vendor

**Score 1–3: Fails the Vendor**

- Missing the property address or giving only a nickname ("the Tracy property").
- Work description is vague — vendor arrives not knowing what the problem is.
- No access instructions — vendor cannot get in.
- No deadline or priority level.
- No contact for questions.
- Email is a paragraph of narrative when a structured list would serve better.

What the vendor feels: I need to call Crown Key before I can even schedule this. Inefficient. May delay the work.

**Score 4–6: Safe but Mediocre**

- Address is included but other details require a follow-up.
- Work is described but access instructions are missing or vague.
- Timeline is present but unclear ("next week sometime").
- Tenant contact information is missing if tenant access is required.
- Invoice instructions not provided.

What the vendor feels: I have most of what I need. Probably one follow-up call required.

**Score 7–8: Good — Vendor Can Act Without Follow-Up**

- Full address, work description, access instructions, timeline, and invoice contact are all present.
- Priority level is stated (Urgent / Standard / Scheduled).
- Confirmation requested: "Please reply to confirm receipt and your availability."
- Organized in a scannable format — not a paragraph wall.

What the vendor feels: complete work order. I can schedule this right now.

**Score 9–10: World Class — Zero-Call Work Order**

All of the above, plus:
- Relevant context that helps the vendor do better work ("Previous repair by [company] — may want to inspect X while on site").
- Specific authorization language so vendor can proceed without additional approval calls.
- Completion reporting instructions are explicit ("Text [number] when done, invoice to [email], reference WO #[X]").
- If tenant access is required, vendor has tenant's name, phone, and preferred contact method.
- Any known complications or access restrictions are flagged upfront.

What the vendor feels: Crown Key runs a tight ship. Easy company to work with. Will prioritize their jobs.

---

### Vendor Score — Dimension Examples

**Completeness (1–10):**
- 1: "Can you fix something at one of our houses?" (no address, no description)
- 5: Address and work description present; access and invoice instructions missing.
- 10: Address, work description, access method, tenant contact (if needed), priority, deadline, authorization, invoice instructions, completion reporting — all present.

**Structure (1–10):**
- 1: One long paragraph.
- 5: Multiple paragraphs with some organization.
- 10: Labeled sections or a structured list — reads like a professional work order.

## Overall Scoring Summary

| Score | Tenant | Owner | Vendor |
|-------|--------|-------|--------|
| 1–3 | Cold, alarming, or confusing — tenant feels like a number | Reactive, no recommendation — owner doubts Crown Key | Key info missing — vendor must call before scheduling |
| 4–6 | Safe but forgettable — does the job, builds no relationship | Reports accurately but no expert value-add | Most info present but at least one gap |
| 7–8 | Professional quality — tenant feels respected and clear | Expert positioning evident — owner confident | Complete work order — no follow-up needed |
| 9–10 | Relationship-building — tenant feels genuinely cared for | Investment-grade communication — owner feels in expert hands | Zero-call, zero-ambiguity work order |
