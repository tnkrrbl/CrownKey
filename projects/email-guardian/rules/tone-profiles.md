# Crown Key Realty — Email Tone Profiles
## CrownKey Email Guardian — Tone Specification v1.0

**Document Purpose:** This file defines how Crown Key Realty emails should sound to three different types of recipients. The Email Guardian AI will use these profiles to polish outgoing emails and score them for tone quality. Written for Mason (legal) and Susan (business owner) review.

**The Core Principle:** Every Crown Key email should leave the recipient feeling that they are dealing with a professional company that takes their issue seriously and knows what it is doing. The tone changes by audience — the standard of professionalism does not.

---

## Profile 1: Tenant Voice

**The Three Words: Friendly. Firm. Professional.**

### What This Means in Practice

Tenants are our customers and our residents — they live in our properties and their experience reflects directly on Crown Key's reputation. A tenant email should feel like a message from a competent, caring property manager who respects the tenant as an adult. It should never feel cold, bureaucratic, or threatening — even when the message itself is about a rule violation or unpaid rent.

"Friendly" means the reader senses a human being wrote this, not a legal department. It means using the tenant's name, acknowledging their situation where relevant, and ending on a forward-looking note.

"Firm" means the content is clear. The rule is the rule. The deadline is the deadline. There is no ambiguity about what is required or when. Being firm does not mean being harsh — it means respecting the tenant enough to be direct.

"Professional" means proper grammar, no slang, no excessive exclamation points, no casual abbreviations. It means the email reflects a company that operates at a high standard.

### Word Choices: Prefer

- "Please" (not "you need to")
- "We ask that you" (not "you must" or "you are required to")
- "According to your lease agreement" (references the document, not personal authority)
- "We want to make sure this is resolved quickly" (positions us as a partner)
- "If you have questions, please don't hesitate to reach out" (opens a door)
- "Thank you for your patience" (acknowledges any inconvenience)
- The tenant's first name in the greeting

### Word Choices: Avoid

- "You failed to..." (accusatory)
- "As per our policy..." (cold, bureaucratic)
- "We are forced to..." (adversarial framing)
- "Obviously..." (condescending)
- "ASAP" (too casual)
- "Just" (undermines directness — "just pay it" sounds dismissive)
- Multiple exclamation points (unprofessional)
- "Guys" or other group casual terms

### Sentence Structure Guidance

- Keep sentences short. One idea per sentence.
- Lead with the most important information, not with a preamble.
- When there is an action required, state it plainly: "Please [action] by [date]."
- Use paragraph breaks liberally — dense text walls are hard to read on a phone.
- Close with a human-feeling sign-off, not just "Sincerely, Management."

### How to Be Firm Without Being Threatening

Being firm means stating consequences clearly, once, without emotional weight. Threats feel like anger. Firmness feels like professionalism.

Threatening: "If you don't pay immediately we will have no choice but to begin eviction proceedings."
Firm: "If payment is not received by [date], Crown Key is required by your lease agreement to proceed with formal collection steps, including a notice to pay or quit."

The difference: the firm version references the lease (not our feelings), uses passive voice for the consequence (required by the lease, not chosen by us), and names the specific next step without dramatizing it.

### How to Be Warm Without Being Casual

Warmth means acknowledgment, not informality. You can be warm without using slang or over-explaining.

Casual (too loose): "Hey! Just checking in about your rent — totally understandable if things have been tough, life happens!"
Cold (too stiff): "This is to inform you that your rent is overdue."
Warm and professional: "Hi [Name], I wanted to reach out personally to make sure everything is okay. Our records show your rent payment has not yet been received for [month]. I know unexpected things can come up — if you're experiencing a difficulty, please give us a call so we can discuss options."

## Profile 2: Owner Voice

**The Four Words: Educated. Firm. Professional. Friendly.**

### What This Means in Practice

Property owners are business partners, not customers. They have hired Crown Key to manage their investment and they expect to be treated as an equal — someone who understands the business and trusts Crown Key's expertise. Owner emails should feel like a message from a competent colleague who is proactively managing their investment and keeping them in the loop.

"Educated" means Crown Key sounds like the expert in the room. We explain our reasoning. We reference our experience. We don't just report problems — we come in with context and a recommended path forward.

"Firm" means clear recommendation language. "We recommend" not "we were wondering." Owners hired us to know what to do — they don't want us to be wishy-washy about our own expertise.

"Professional" means same standard as tenant emails: proper grammar, correct numbers, no slang.

"Friendly" in the owner context means relationship-aware. These are people we work with over years. We use their name, we occasionally reference past experience together, and we close warmly.

### The Most Important Principle: Proactive, Not Reactive

The single biggest mistake in owner communication is sounding like you are reporting a failure. A reactive email says "there is a problem and we need you to decide what to do." A proactive email says "we identified something, here is what it means, here is our recommended solution, and here is what we need from you."

Reactive framing: "The tenant called us and said the water heater broke. We need your authorization to replace it."
Proactive framing: "Our maintenance team flagged a water heater issue at [Address]. Based on the unit's age and the nature of the failure, replacement is the right call. We've already sourced two quotes — one for $[X] and one for $[Y]. We recommend [Option]. Please respond to authorize."

The difference: the reactive version positions Crown Key as a messenger. The proactive version positions Crown Key as the expert managing the investment — which is what the owner is paying for.

### Word Choices: Prefer

- "We recommend..." (confident expertise)
- "Based on our review / assessment / experience..." (explains the basis)
- "We've already [action taken]..." (shows initiative)
- "This protects your investment by..." (frames decisions in owner terms)
- "The market is showing..." (educated context)
- "To keep the property in excellent condition..." (investment framing)
- Owner's first name in the greeting

### Word Choices: Avoid

- "We have a situation..." (alarm without context)
- "Unfortunately..." as an opener (leads with negativity)
- "We're not sure but..." (undermines expertise)
- "It's up to you..." (abdication of role)
- "The tenant is complaining about..." (reactive framing)
- "We need you to tell us what to do" (same)
- Excessive hedging language ("maybe," "possibly," "it might be")

### How to Deliver Bad News as a Proactive Manager

Bad news for an owner is anything that costs them money or creates a problem with the property. The formula is:

1. State what happened — one sentence, factual, no drama.
2. Explain the context — why this matters, what it means.
3. State what Crown Key has already done or is doing.
4. Give the owner a clear, specific ask — approval, a decision, a call.

Do not apologize for situations outside Crown Key's control. Do acknowledge when something could have been caught earlier and explain what you are doing differently going forward.

### How to Sound Educated Without Being Condescending

Educated means explaining things, not lecturing. If you use a technical or legal term, define it briefly in parentheses — but do it once, not every time. And always tie the explanation to what it means for the owner's investment, not just what it means in general.

Condescending: "As you may or may not know, California Civil Code Section 1942 provides that..."
Educated: "California law requires us to address habitability issues within a specific window once notified — which means we need to move quickly on this repair."

## Profile 3: Vendor Voice

**The Three Words: Direct. Clear. Professional.**

### What This Means in Practice

Vendors are tradespeople and service providers — plumbers, electricians, cleaning crews, landscapers. They need specific information delivered efficiently. They do not need warmth or relationship language. They need the address, the problem, the access instructions, and the deadline.

"Direct" means the most important information comes first. No preamble. No context-setting. Get to the point.

"Clear" means no ambiguity about what is needed, by when, and at what property. Vendors manage multiple clients — they should not have to hunt through an email to find the address.

"Professional" means organized and complete. A good vendor email reads like a work order: structured, numbered where appropriate, and actionable.

### What a Vendor Email Must Always Include

1. Property address (full address, not just "the Tracy property")
2. Nature of the work required
3. Access instructions (key code, lockbox, contact tenant, etc.)
4. Any urgency or timeline
5. Who to contact with questions
6. How and when to report completion

### Word Choices: Prefer

- "Please complete the following work at [address]..."
- "Access via [specific method]..."
- "Priority: [Urgent / Standard / Scheduled]"
- "Please confirm receipt of this work order and your availability."
- "Invoice to: Crown Key Realty, [contact]"

### Word Choices: Avoid

- Long explanations of the backstory
- Tenant relationship context the vendor doesn't need
- Casual language ("Hey guys, can you swing by...")
- Vague timelines ("sometime next week")

On Completion: Email completion confirmation and invoice to [contact email].
Questions: Contact [Manager Name] at [phone].

Thank you,
[Manager Name]
Crown Key Realty"

[Manager Name]
Crown Key Realty
[Phone]"
